## Post 1
**Topic:** Mirrors vs mirroring — feeling seen vs transparency

People don't want the "truth" reflected back in some abstract way. They want their experience to be recognized. They want their perspective to matter. Shifting my thinking from mirrors to mirroring has been a big mental leap. It is the shift from showing people the truth to helping people feel seen. And in the current landscape, feeling "seen" might actually look like the reverse of transparency. It is someone feeling like their perspective is understood, but is not being held against them.

## Post 2
**Topic:** Healthy AI skepticism as commitment to outcome

Healthy AI skepticism is not negativity. It is one of the ways people show commitment to an outcome.

Skepticism does not mean "I want this to fail."

It means: "The outcome here matters enough that we should get this right."

Healthy skepticism IS a form of optimism. Not the optimism that says, "This will definitely work," but the optimism that says, "This could work, and it matters enough to examine carefully."

It is also a form of care for what already exists: for what might be working, for what we should remember is working, and for what has worked before.

In a situation where so many people are breathlessly excited and vibing, you need skeptics to keep things grounded. The more powerful the wave, the more important it becomes to ask what is true and what is working.

I think about my favorite recording engineers. They are not sitting in the back of the studio dismissing new tools. They are rolling up their sleeves, trying things, listening, and testing the results against what they know works.

They do not treat their tools as magic. Their default is often, "We probably do not need this." But that does not keep them from experimenting.

That is the kind of skepticism we need more of. Involved skepticism. Craft skepticism. The kind that cares enough about the outcome to know when a new tool is actually making the work better.

But let's go back to the first point:

Healthy AI skepticism is not negativity. It is one of the ways people show commitment to an outcome.

That is the crux question, I guess. What is the outcome?

If the outcome is "take advantage of the hype while the hype is hot," then skepticism will probably feel like negativity. It slows things down. It asks annoying questions. It threatens momentum.

But maybe that is exactly the problem.

If the outcome is to build useful products, make better decisions, improve work, protect customers, avoid waste, and understand what these tools can and cannot do, then skepticism is not a drag on the work. It is part of the work.

If not….

## Post 3
**Topic:** 20 things in product/tech that don't change because of AI

Here are 20 things in product/tech that don't change because of AI

Market/Customer Dynamics

1. It is hard to predict what people will adopt, because behavior change depends on context, incentives, trust, timing, and switching costs.

2. Alternatives always compete to meet the same need: competitors, existing tools, workarounds, habits, + doing nothing

3. Customers seek better value, where "better" may mean cheaper, easier, safer, faster, more trusted, or less disruptive

4. Preferences and expectations keep shifting, even when underlying needs remain stable

5. Entrenched solutions have inertia, "good enough" can be a moat, and timing often determines whether a better product wins

Strategic Allocation / Scale

6. There are always constraints on how many things you can meaningfully try, learn from, and support, even if the constraint shifts

7. Product teams balance horizons: improving what exists, adapting to change, and exploring what could make the current product obsolete

8. Companies and investors decide how much capital goes toward exploring the new, expanding what works, sustaining what exists, and extracting value

9. You will always have more ideas than customers can absorb, buyers will pay for, and your company can package, maintain+support

10. Companies can only sustain so many motions before coordination and coherence costs slow them down. There are physics of focus

Market Structure / Value Capture

11. Markets cycle through bundling and unbundling, forcing teams and investors to decide whether the opportunity is a focused product, workflow, platform, embedded feature, or suite

12. Product teams navigate vertical specificity versus horizontal breadth: sharper fit and trust versus larger markets and reusable leverage

13. Products sit inside value chains, where value is created, passed along, captured, compressed, or redirected across companies, platforms, channels, and end customers

14. Technology markets move through boom, bubble, + bust cycles, creating survival challenges and opportunities in each phase

15. Technology markets organize into layers, with cornerstone technologies underneath and shifting battles over who captures value

Craft/Expertise

16. The technology landscape keeps evolving, so product teams must reassess what is newly possible, cheap, expected, or threatened

17. Technology creates tension between encapsulating domain skill and amplifying it: replacing expertise, making experts more powerful, helping novices, or shifting where expertise matters

18. Great products require integrating multiple disciplines, perspectives, and tensions, whether the work is done by humans, AI, or both

19. Product strategy is a dynamic game, not a static optimization problem: rules change, competitors respond, customers adapt, and companies can shape the future they are trying to win in

20. Design bridges imagined futures, current realities, and the affordances that make behaviors easier, harder, more likely, or less likely

**Topic:** Shipping faster vs learning faster — which is the real bottleneck?

"Are you shipping faster than you learn? Or learning faster than you ship?"

I remember when Tanner McGrath posed this question almost eight years ago, before AI was capturing everyone's attention.

At the time, I would encounter companies that fit squarely in the "learning faster" category. But not as many as you would think.

This is what gives me pause when people talk about the bottleneck of shipping. Even then, a majority of teams had a learning bottleneck….not a shipping bottleneck.

## Post 5
**Topic:** Power dynamics — leaders who forget others work around them

Many leaders spent years learning to work around power on the way up. At the top, they forget others are now working around them.

That gap is where a wicked cycle starts.

A leader holds real power. Not symbolic power. Actual power over what gets rewarded, ignored, punished, and worked around.

People around them adapt. They hold back. They route around. They say the safe thing, or say nothing at all.

The leader notices. And resents it.

"Why won't anyone just be straight with me?"

They'll often complain that the environment has become political. Too much triangulation and careful phrasing. Not enough "straight talk." People strategizing instead of collaborating.

What they rarely connect is that they are the source of that.

When power is uneven, "politics" is not a personality flaw in the culture. It is a reasonable adaptation to the person in the room.

What they often miss is that the caution they're seeing is a rational response to the asymmetry they hold. Reitz and Higgins put this well: leaders underestimate how their power silences others, and overestimate how much candid feedback they actually receive.

But here's the part that makes the loop wicked.

The resentment doesn't stop at "people aren't being honest with me." It morphs into something worse. Because people are caught in the power dynamic, the leader starts to think less of them. Timid. Political. Unable to speak up. Not leadership material. Not the kind of person who "tells it like it is."

The irony....They resent the politics. They also produce it. On the way up, power was an external force to navigate, game, and surmount. They rarely carry forward the mirror image.

Which helps explain two familiar patterns. They look externally for new people, hoping to find straight talk somewhere outside the culture they helped create. And they get preoccupied with rewarding their mirror image as it rises through the org. The person who reminds them of who they were, or wish they still were. Not the people working around them, the way they once worked around someone else. So the cycle tightens.

-Power silences people.
-Silence frustrates the leader.
-Frustration becomes contempt.
-Contempt makes people quieter, more political, more careful.
-The leader resents how "political" everything has become.
-The organization learns to work around them.
-The leader feels more isolated, more resentful, more certain the problem is everyone else's character.

And the official culture keeps insisting the door is open. This is why "speak up" initiatives so often fail. You're not fixing a courage problem. You're inside a loop.

The hard question isn't "how do we get people to be braver?"

It's whether the people with power can see themselves as part of the dynamic they're complaining about.

And whether they're willing to ask what their own position is teaching everyone else about what's safe to say.

## Post 6
**Topic:** AI supercharging avoidance of collaboration — meetings matter more now

I care more about meetings and collaboration now than I ever have before. AI is the culprit. Why?

People with a bias toward working alone suddenly have a whole new quiver of tactics to avoid working with other people. They set up agents. They send copious documentation. Their agents talk to your agents. They shut down conversations with hastily pulled together, but very persuasive, deflectors.

I'm not saying this is intentional or malicious. But if you're tuned in a certain direction, AI can accentuate that tuning.

If they were allergic to "groupthink," and they now exist in a world where agents do their bidding, then the future is now.

People who see value in collaboration are now overloaded trying to handle the overflow of decision-making, noise, and responses to all of these async tactics.

They are desperate to be thoughtful while dealing with this deluge of theoretically replaceable moments.

There's the potential for way more WIP, and people only have marginal control over it, though they imagine their agent-form does. It sits in their mind, eating away at their focus.

Good collaboration can save millions of dollars of wasted effort.

It can be one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

Like a lot of things, AI can supercharge "bad" habits, or existing habits, no judgment, or it can supercharge adaptation to the new reality.

## Post 7
**Topic:** Team definition — multiple lenses, not one dominant view

So why is team definition so hard?

"What is a team?" is actually an easy question. It's a group of people who collaborate. A workgroup is a temporary team. It's when team = product, or team = promotion, or team = value that you get into (necessary and productive) trouble.

I think of it in terms of lenses.

A collaboration lens (who actually works together day to day). A value lens (what value does this group deliver to customers). A technology lens (what systems and services does this group own). A mandate lens (what has this group been asked to do, what authority do they have).

And there are others. A financial lens (how does this group map to revenue streams or cost centers). A market lens (how does this group align to customer segments or competitive positioning, which hopefully maps to the value architecture… but maybe not).

These lenses overlap the concept of a team, but they aren't fundamentally about teams or collaboration. Still, the power of those lenses has a big impact on how people discuss teaming.

All of these lenses are valuable. But what we see at Dotwork is that there's a battle for the "dominant" lens. People are so used to jamming whatever they're doing into a single tool or slide deck that it becomes prohibitive for multiple lenses to coexist.

So you get the "official lens" of the moment, and the other groups keep shadow systems: capability trees in Miro, growth flywheels, collaboration agreements, product taxonomies.

One of the big challenges with the push to product centricity is that you can't snap your fingers and restructure the org into perfectly aligned, independent teams. You can't magically turn every team into a full-stack team. You'll have platforms, components, teams wearing four hats across different "topologies." PMs sharing engineering teams. Single PMs serving too many engineering teams. Despite the in-theory soundness of cross-functional teams, that may not extend to the engineering org chart. You may have trouble building truly fractal triads and layers.

And so you see a pendulum. Swing toward product-centric definitions, and you mask collaboration complexity. Lean into collaboration and coordination complexity, and you lose sight of the big picture and value. The shadow system of the day depends on where you are in the swing.

Describing these lenses is not technically difficult. It doesn't require a lot of skill. That isn't the hard part. The hard part is navigating the tension between the lenses and the implications for groups and teams.

The definition of a team is simple. The hard part is letting multiple lenses coexist without one flattening the others.

## Post 8
**Topic:** AI shines brightest with boring tech — use your bleeding-edge budget wisely

AI often shines brightest with boring tech. Boring tech is time-tested, documented, observable, debuggable, maintainable. Boring in the best possible way.

"Use boring tech" has always been popular advice, but I think it is even more important now. Because when you add something probabilistic, you want the surrounding system to be as legible, observable, and dependable as possible.

It doesn't mean use things that are outdated. It means look for the most modern thing that simply does the job.

Your "bleeding edge budget" should go somewhere, and it isn't to scratch your itch of using the newest thing at every layer of the stack.

## Post 9
**Topic:** AI culture shift narrative is demotivating — name behaviors, not culture

Enough with the "AI is a culture shift" narrative! Seriously. I'm not sure leaders realize how massively demotivating the phrase "change the culture" can be for employees, especially right now in relation to AI.

First, when you're overwhelming people with layoffs, mandates, platitudes, and hand-waving, alluding to culture holds no water.

Second, "culture" can function like a dog whistle in so many ways.

It often sounds positive and collective on the surface, but employees may hear something very different: "You are the problem," "You need to adapt," "You need to be more committed," or "The old way of working is no longer acceptable." Instead of naming specific behaviors, incentives, decisions, or trade-offs, "culture" can become a vague container for blame.

Third, no one is really talking about shifting power balances or changing the underlying belief systems that shape the organization.

So when leaders say "change the culture," it can sound hollow. Employees hear a call for them to behave differently, while the deeper structures, incentives, decision rights, and assumptions remain untouched.

Fourth, there is the basic "what's in it for me?" question

The message often lands as: "You all need to change." But why? Toward what end? For whose benefit? Without a clear answer, "culture change" becomes a proxy for "new behaviors, everyone — and if you don't adapt, you may lose your job."

Fifth, "culture" is a big, overused word in corporate settings.

Yes, companies develop norms, habits, rituals, language, incentives, and shared assumptions. But calling that "culture" can overstate what work is and ask too much of employees.

A company culture is not the same as the deeper cultures people inhabit outside of work: their families, communities, histories, identities, and lived experiences. So when leaders talk about "changing the culture," it can feel grandiose, invasive, or even manipulative, as if the company is asking people to remake themselves rather than simply clarifying how work needs to change.

Sixth, let's be honest: "culture" often becomes a setup for "good fit" / "bad fit" conversations without leaders being clear about the norms they actually expect. Instead of saying, "Here are the behaviors we need, here is why they matter, and here is how we will support people in making the shift," it can become a vague judgment of whether someone belongs.

## Post 11
**Topic:** "Move faster with AI" = delayed institutional approval of work previously ignored

Congratulations to all the teams who are now being told to "move faster with AI" by finally being allowed to do, with institutional enthusiasm, some version of what they were asking to do five years ago.

It is an incredible moment!

After years of being told there was no time to:

-learn
-improve inputs
-have any real appetite for documentation
-fund research ops
-maintain context
-reduce coordination drag
-recognize the people doing synthesis

...we have finally discovered the answer: AI.

At long last, orgs can embrace the radical idea that better organized information, clear thinking, reusable context, and less wasted effort might improve performance. Crazy!?!

A team could have spent years saying: we need

-shared context
-stronger research habits
-better ways to connect decisions to execution

And that all sounded soft. Operational. Process-y. Glue-y. When layoffs came, maybe even like a sign of weakness... not the kind of work anyone was lining up to defend.

But run the exact same argument through the phrase "with AI," and suddenly MAGIC. Now the same org that rolled its eyes at context management is deeply interested in context "engineering."

The same leaders who did not want to fund internal scaffolding are very eager to invest in AI readiness.

same teams that got no credit for synthesis are being told that synthesis is now a strategic capability. MAGIC!

Amazing what a little relabeling can do.

...We can now pretend something entirely new is happening.

A lot of "AI acceleration" is just the delayed institutional approval of work that was previously ignored. Which is fine, of course. Honestly, if the only way to get orgs to value these things is to smuggle them in under an AI banner... okay, sure.

But we should at least admit the joke.

In many cases, "now with AI" does not mean teams are suddenly doing impossible new things. But what keeps reappearing inside the shiny new future looks suspiciously like writing, glue work, synthesis, decision support, research prep, organizational memory, and being a "learning organization."

But this still leaves companies with a choice.

They can finally value the work they used to ignore.
Support it.
Incentivize it.
Recognize that synthesis, context management, careful framing, and better coordination were never fake work just because they were hard to measure

Or something much uglier.

They can
-use AI as a smokescreen for pure extraction.
-strip-mine people's judgment, habits, notes, patterns, and tacit knowledge, while still refusing to reward the underlying labor.
-declare victory because the outputs look faster, while the actual benefits continue flowing upward, outward, or into someone else's margins.

That is the real fork in the road

Not whether AI changes the work. It obviously does. The real q is whether this becomes a moment where companies finally respect and invest in the kinds of work they spent years undervaluing... or whether they just found a slick way to extract more from it.
