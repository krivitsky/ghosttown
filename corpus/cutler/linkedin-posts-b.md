## Post 1 (2023)
**Topic:** Prioritization requires trade-offs
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_you-cant-prioritize-something-unless-you-activity-7098425693218226177-3893

You can't prioritize something unless you deprioritize something else. Otherwise, it isn't a priority, it's just another task on the list. There's nothing wrong with tasks—they're just not priorities. To avoid this, try this prompt: When faced with the decision of whether to spend time and energy __________ or to invest their time and energy in ________________, we are encouraging the team to prioritize the latter over the former. Example: When faced with the decision of whether to spend time and energy exclusively on acquiring new customers or to invest their time and energy in retaining and deepening relationships with our existing customer base, we are encouraging the team to prioritize the latter over the former.

## Post 2 (2023)
**Topic:** Breaking out of "peanut butter" strategy cycles
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_it-can-be-very-hard-to-break-this-cycle-activity-6895218644364742657-SoWF

It can be very hard to break this cycle. There's no sugar-coating the situation. When nothing is really "working" (or when it is hard to understand what is working, or when there's a lot of success theater, and people sense that)...your only option is to go broad, and settle for ineffective proxies. Pressure increases. You're focused on being "responsive", saying yes, the optics of a "solid plan", and focusing on things like predictability and doing what you said you promised to do. You end up with a peanut butter strategy. That in turn negatively impacts outcomes and your ability to measure outcomes. Lo and behold you are back at being unclear about what is working. How do you break out of this? Anyone who tells you "just ___" is fooling themselves. The trick is having something WORK. For real. Success invites success. How to carve out the room to make this happen has no playbook. It is very culturally specific.

## Post 4 (2023)
**Topic:** What PMs do when they don't write specs
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_a-product-manager-who-doesnt-write-specs-activity-6879470782402187264-sx2r

A product manager who doesn't write specs/stories/tickets requires (at minimum):
1. Engineers with bandwidth to participate in work breakdown
2. Less pressure for "premature convergence" on details
3. Engineers empowered with product-centric incentives
4. Collaborative problem-solving from the beginning
5. Adequate design resources
6. Direct customer access across the team
7. Shared ownership of outcomes (not siloed responsibilities)
8. Alternative documentation focusing on problem context
9. Prototypes serving as working specifications
10. Mission-driven work rather than transactional task management

When PMs aren't mired in spec writing, they can focus on gathering data/context about the actual problem. Spec-writing often stems from approval processes and estimation needs rather than genuine product clarity, creating unnecessary bureaucracy in mature product teams.

## Post 5 (2023)
**Topic:** What makes a good strategy (healthy dissonance)
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_a-good-strategy-encourages-a-level-of-healthy-activity-6993317859686830080-OULu

A good strategy encourages a level of *healthy* dissonance and tension (at first). A bad strategy strategy encourages *unhealthy* dissonance and tension OR no dissonance at all. Let me explain the difference: If you read a description of a strategy and end up nodding and saying "yeah sure", then the strategy isn't doing its job. The best strategies create an instant spark of curiosity and tension. "Wait, are we sure?" "Woah. That's powerful. I have to think on that." "I'm going to have to think of the ramifications." "That took me a second...now it is starting to make sense." "That's going to require a lot of discipline. It's a shift." "If that could work, it would make things a lot easier...." "Wow. There are a couple layers to that statement!" What's going on here? Good strategies get people's brain MOVING. They get people thinking. They are often counterintuitive. If everyone nods their head, you probably need to work on your strategy. If you get a million *good* questions, you are getting warmer. Look for the spark.

Product is hard, but most product teams don't appreciate how good they have it compared to groups and individuals in customer success, support, and marketing. ## Post 7 (2023)
**Topic:** FAB framework for team alignment (Facts, Assumptions, Beliefs)
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_an-activity-any-team-can-do-to-stay-more-activity-6982164792987852800-GBM3

An activity ANY team can do to stay more aligned — FAB: Facts, Assumptions, Beliefs.

Facts: List incontrovertible FACTS relevant to your work — specific metrics, competitive moves, observable realities.

Assumptions: Document operating assumptions with confidence levels and implications. Indicate the current level of certainty and relationship to ongoing work, categorizing them as either "Operating" or "Testing" types.

Beliefs: These are foundational assumptions spanning longer timeframes — years and/or decades — acknowledging contrarian and untestable assumptions about your market, customers, and world.

Establish a ritual of regularly revisiting your FAB while maintaining documentation history. The framework scales fractally across organizational levels. Company-wide FABs, and team-level FABs, should ideally be public and accessible.

## Post 8 (2023)
**Topic:** How to become more predictable as a product team
**URL:** https://me.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_how-to-become-more-predictable-as-a-product-activity-7026095154667130880-Fi4_

How to become "more predictable" as a product team:
* Work smaller
* Work together
* Work on less at once
* Start finishing, stop starting
* Integrate continuously
* Get feedback early and often
* Let the design emerge
* Focus on flow — adapt scope to available time
* Celebrate achievements
* Build in recovery time
* Visualize workflow and bottlenecks

Don't worry about predictability. It is a byproduct.

Avoid: Big-batches, siloed work, overloading quarters like a Tetris board, pursuing new initiatives when blocked, delayed integration, treating work as proprietary, rapid convergence on plans, upstream design freezes, and heavy estimation efforts. Don't make predictability the goal — it typically backfires.

## Post 11 (2023)
**Topic:** Effective internal product teams at Toast
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnpcutler_during-my-first-couple-weeks-at-toast-i-have-activity-7041993142962716673-9YqB

During my first couple weeks at Toast I have been thinking a great deal about what makes effective internal product teams. Five key characteristics (developed in partnership with Nick Reynolds):

1. Capability-Aligned Teams: Rather than organizing around projects, advocate for small, stable, and capability-aligned teams focused on long-term improvement of specific abilities.

2. Value-First Approach: Teams should "nail it, then scale it" — prioritizing demonstrating value before worrying about architecture or cost optimization.

3. Pull Over Push: Effective teams act as teachers, ambassadors, evangelists, and advocates, creating genuine demand rather than forcing adoption.

4. Ecosystem Perspective: Internal product teams must understand how their work serves a web of services and capabilities benefiting the entire organization.

5. Strategic Alignment: Teams should deeply understand and shape the company strategy, enabling new options rather than building for its own sake.

Experience and usability matter. The ultimate goal is value creation and problem-solving — not consistency for its own sake.

## Post 14 (2025, June)
**Topic:** Thriving during the Great Flattening — org design for fewer management layers
**URL:** https://www.dotwork.com/post/thriving-during-the-great-flattening

The "Great Flattening" describes organizations with fewer management layers, higher leader-to-report ratios, and competing demands to improve efficiency while driving innovation and adopting AI. Eight actionable strategies for leaders:

1. Audit Planning Cycles: Organizations waste substantial resources on ritual preparation. One company estimated quarterly planning at "$4,000,000 per quarter in salary time alone." Eliminate low-value prep work and streamline data collection.

2. Identify Perverse Incentives: Teams pursuing efficiency may inadvertently create problems — chasing false precision, excessive multitasking to maximize utilization, or performative micromanagement. Fear-based cultures discourage transparency about blockers.

3. Surface "No Win" Tradeoffs: Rather than escalating decisions up the chain, use prioritization heuristics to enable faster decision-making and prevent diluted efforts attempting to accomplish everything simultaneously.

4. Consolidate Rituals: Replace numerous small meetings and 1:1s with fewer, well-designed gatherings that include proper preparation materials and shared context.

5. Strengthen Feedback Channels: Create safe environments for surfacing friction. Currently, fear and reorganization fatigue suppress honest communication about problems.

6. Build Conviction Around Actionable Inputs: Teams need 3-5 metrics they directly influence to maintain momentum and enable tight feedback loops for learning.

7. Clarify Platform Strategy: Platform investments require intentional strategy and consistent nurturing to unlock efficiency gains across the organization.

8. Distinguish Operational vs. Capital Efficiency: Operational efficiency means doing more with less; capital efficiency means allocating resources to achieve long-term strategic goals. Both matter differently in the current economic environment.

## Post 15 (2026, June)
**Topic:** AI adoption and employee agency
**URL:** https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-425-ai-and-agency

So you want your team to figure out how to use AI for the benefit of your company? Then stop stripping them of the agency to do it.

The language sounds like "this is for your growth," but the structure says "this is for our Q3 goals."

Years of layoffs, reorgs, broken promises... now you're surprised they don't take "this will be great for you" at face value?

Give me a real problem, give me space to figure out whether AI helps solve it, and get out of my way.

Prescriptive technologies "eliminate the occasions for decision-making and judgment" (via Ursula Franklin). Leaders driving AI adoption while trampling agency, trust, and dignity are using prescriptive technology logic against their own people. Many leadership teams treat resistance to AI as a personal failing rather than a rational response to changing environments.

Three motivational needs being undermined by mandated AI adoption (Self-Determination Theory):
- Autonomy: Directives replace choice
- Competence: Existing skills appear devalued
- Relatedness: Individual "superpower" narratives undermine collaboration

Teams that win in 2026 will treat AI as a measurable investment, plan with economic discipline, and design their orgs to run AI products, not experiments.
