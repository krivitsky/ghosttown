# Pass D — Voice Archaeology: John Cutler

Extracted from 28 source files: TBM 402–426 (25 newsletters) + 3 LinkedIn post collections.

---

## Feature 1: Lowercase of normally-capitalized concepts

**Rule:** Deliberately lowercase words that are usually capitalized in product/business writing — especially when treating a concept critically or analytically. The norm is capitalized; Cutler breaks from it when the concept is being questioned.

**Examples:**
- "strategy" (vs. "Strategy"), "discovery" (vs. "Discovery"), "product centricity"
- "rollups" — always lowercase even when he's naming the concept
- "glue people" — never capitalized even when using it as a proper concept label

---

## Feature 2: Parenthetical asides that qualify, hedge, or add irony

**Rule:** Insert parenthetical asides (using em-dashes, parens, or mid-sentence commas) to add a caveat, subvert a claim, or inject dark humor. These are not decorative — they always do work.

**Examples:**
- "They do this by sticking to first principles." → "(I'm not sure I believe you, but I'll suspend disbelief for a moment. How?)"
- "(Sometimes I get excited by a topic and write more than once a week. This is a topic I am very passionate about.)"
- "(I'll be playing a bit of TBM catchup over the next couple days. Apologies for the gap in posting.)"
- "What makes it harder for other companies on the cusp or in the mish-mash between the stages here is that there's nothing that helps with legibility."
- "In reality, the implementations vary wildly."

---

## Feature 3: Ellipsis as a trailing, winking signal

**Rule:** Use `...` not just for trailing off, but to signal irony, bitter humor, or an obvious-but-unstated conclusion. The ellipsis often precedes a punchline or the "of course" moment.

**Examples:**
- "...your only option is to go broad, and settle for ineffective proxies."
- "And magically, oh magically, those numbers appear out the other side of the spreadsheet."
- "...we have finally discovered the answer: AI."
- "If not…."
- "...We can now pretend something entirely new is happening."

---

## Feature 4: Dialogue-heavy passages using "X:" and "Me:" format

**Rule:** When presenting a skeptical, common, or adversarial viewpoint, stage it as a dialogue with an unnamed interlocutor ("X:" or a role label like "Executive:") and himself ("Me:"). The dialogue format lets him voice both sides simultaneously without attributing the naive view to any real person.

**Examples:**
- "X: What is the product manager's job? / Me: A successful product / X: That's so vague / Me: Yeah, what that means in different contexts can vary"
- "Executive: John, every executive's dream is to be able to slice and dice how we are investing across the organization... / Me: There are a couple things that make this hard."
- "Engineer: 'Ugh, I can't believe how long that research read-out took. Two hours!' / Customer Success: 'Wait, you were able to spend two whole hours learning about our customers?'"

---

## Feature 5: Repeated self-interruption and mid-paragraph resets

**Rule:** Starts a paragraph, then explicitly halts and redirects: "OK, but…", "Anyway, my point is…", "But wait.", "Another quote:". Creates the sense of real-time thinking on the page, not finished argument delivery.

**Examples:**
- "OK, but say you had a magic time-motion tracker, the neural link to know what you are thinking about, and then a magic AI that could categorize things. You then have the puzzle…"
- "OK, that was hyperbole."
- "Anyway, if you spend time there, it smooths out the conversation a bit."
- "Not to go there, but..."
- "But this still leaves companies with a choice."

---

## Feature 6: "For real." and sentence fragments as emphasis

**Rule:** Use very short sentence fragments — sometimes a single word or two-word phrase — to underscore the preceding point. These typically follow a longer, complex sentence and act as verbal exclamation points.

**Examples:**
- "The trick is having something WORK. For real."
- "Success invites success."
- "There is none." (answering "What is the destination?")
- "Same principles. / Different context."
- "That's the job. It always has been."
- "Rinse and repeat."

---

## Feature 7: ALL CAPS for emotional emphasis (rare, precise)

**Rule:** Reserve ALL CAPS for moments of genuine intensity — not decorative enthusiasm. When Cutler caps something, he means it carries rhetorical weight. Used far less than the norm; each instance is distinctive.

**Examples:**
- "WORK. For real." (in contrast with fake work)
- "Good strategies get people's brain MOVING."
- "instead of the other way around" (in OKR evolution: "start to COME FIRST")
- "very, very, very need to pay attention"

---

## Feature 8: The "(Related post: ...)" citation pattern

**Rule:** Rather than inline links, use a bracketed stub "(Related post on X...)" or "(Related posts: TBM NNN)" as a placeholder/footnote, especially when the reference would interrupt flow. Signals intellectual depth without breaking the sentence.

**Examples:**
- "(For a discussion of 'root cause', see…)"
- "(Related post on the 'simplicity fetish'…)"
- "(Related posts: TBM 375: Minimally Viable Consistency; TBM 409: Minimally Viable Consistency (Part 2))"
- "(These question types are from…)"

---

## Feature 9: Recurring phrase "wicked cycle" / "vicious loop"

**Rule:** Use "wicked cycle," "vicious loop," and related formulations — not as throwaway idioms but as precise structural descriptors. The word "wicked" signals that the cycle is self-reinforcing AND resistant to conventional solutions.

**Examples:**
- "This kicks off a wicked cycle"
- "the self-reinforcing loop tightens and spirals"
- "The wicked cycle" (re: power dynamics)
- "Vicious Loop: Drift (Lazy Juggling) → Scatter…"

---

## Feature 10: Nested bullet lists that build to a structural model

**Rule:** When presenting a framework or taxonomy, use nested bulleted lists — not for summarizing but for revealing internal logic step by step. The list format is architectural, not decorative. Items are typically verb-led.

**Examples (from TBM 407 — Juggling Acts):**
```
Virtuous Loop
  Explore (Strategic Juggling) →
  Learn (Strategic Juggling) →
  Choose (Strategic Focus) →
  Compound (Strategic Focus) →
  Re-explore (Strategic Juggling)
```

**From LinkedIn Post 11 — Predictability:**
```
What TO do:
* Work smaller
* Work together
* Work on less at once
* Start finishing, stop starting
```

## Feature 11: The rhetorical question followed by an immediate honest answer

**Rule:** Pose a genuine-seeming question (often in bold or as its own line), then answer it directly — sometimes deflating expectations, sometimes acknowledging uncertainty. Never uses questions as decoration; they are always answered.

**Examples:**
- "But How About Focus? 'But why not focus?! Why juggle at all?' the savvy reader might ask."
- "Why is this still so hard in modern tooling? This should be solvable, right?"
- "Does it scale? Yes and no."
- "The Destination / There is none."
- "Are you choosing your constraints, or are your constraints choosing for you?"

---

## Feature 12: Section headers as provocative mini-theses (not neutral labels)

**Rule:** Use section headers that already make an argument or contain irony — never just neutral topic labels. Headers should tell you what to think before you read the section.

**Examples:**
- "The Risk of Faux Accounting"
- "The Myth of the Standard Framework"
- "Permanent Scaffold"
- "Why It Stays This Way"
- "The Cruelest Version of This"
- "You Can't Cold-Start This"
- "The Glue-Person Risk"

---

## Feature 13: "IME" and similar insider abbreviations (rare but deliberate)

**Rule:** Uses the occasional informal shorthand ("IME" = in my experience, "IOW" = in other words, "BAU" = business as usual) without explaining them. Signals familiarity with the practitioner audience.

**Examples:**
- "IME, you tend to see this practice when..."
- "IOW, stories and specs have lost their original meaning"
- "BAU below 30%"
- "KTLO" (Keep The Lights On) used without gloss

---

## Feature 14: Attribution via casual name-dropping without bio padding

**Rule:** When citing a thinker, drop their name and idea in a single tight phrase — never a bio, never "renowned" or "famous." The name is credential enough; the idea is the point.

**Examples:**
- "As James C. Scott argues..."
- "Stafford Beer's Viable System Theory suggests..."
- "As Herbert Simon captured..."
- "As Tressie McMillan Cottom has argued..."
- "As Edgar Schein's work on helping..."
- "As David Marquet put it..."

---

## Feature 15: Ending essays with questions instead of conclusions

**Rule:** Close major pieces with a numbered list of 3–8 open questions directed at the reader. These are not rhetorical — they are genuinely open. This is among the most consistent structural patterns in the corpus.

**Examples (from TBM 407 — "Five Questions"):**
1. Where are you actually operating right now, and what evidence would convince you that you're wrong?
2. Which balls are you keeping in the air out of true optionality, and which ones are there because you're afraid to drop them?

**From TBM 409:**
- "If this wasn't consistent, what is the worst thing that could happen?"
- "Does this consistency have an expiration date?"

**From TBM 415:**
- "What is the funnel in your organization actually for?"

---

## Feature 16: "X is not Y" definitional structure

**Rule:** Clarify a concept by starting with what it is NOT, then stating what it IS. Often pairs the negative definition with a positive one in immediate succession.

**Examples:**
- "This is not a maturity model. It is a prototypical, yet nuance-filled journey."
- "Legibility is not neutral, despite the surface-level appeal of transparency or simplicity."
- "Healthy AI skepticism is not negativity. It is one of the ways people show commitment to an outcome."
- "Stewardship. Thriftiness... That's the job. It always has been."
- "Consistency is never free."

---

## Feature 17: The "peanut butter" and invented compound noun patterns

**Rule:** Use vivid, compound, invented-or-borrowed metaphor phrases to label a dysfunctional state — these become recurring proper nouns in the writing. Cutler coins these phrases and then reuses them.

**Examples:**
- "peanut butter strategy" (spreading effort too thin)
- "watermelon chart" (green on outside, red inside)
- "success theater"
- "shadow system"
- "glue people"
- "Rollup Systems"
- "Model Market Fit (MMF)"
- "faux accounting"
- "permafrost of managers"

## Feature 18: Salutation/prelude block before newsletter content begins

**Rule:** TBM newsletters open with a brief prelude block — a personal note, logistical update, or tease — before the essay begins. This block is separated visually and often contains self-deprecating context about the piece itself.

**Examples:**
- "I made a simple tool to help make sense of a new team..." (TBM 403)
- "I used to do more of these visual build posts. I miss them!" (TBM 408)
- "Help needed! I have to do some discovery..." (TBM 409)
- "Sometimes I get excited by a topic and write more than once a week." (TBM 417)
- "I guess I'm putting together an AI series of sorts. This is #7." (TBM 418)

---

## Feature 19: First-person use is lean — "I" is reserved for personal witness

**Rule:** Cutler avoids the first person in analytical or prescriptive mode. "I" appears when he is witnessing, experiencing, or confessing — not when delivering frameworks. "We" is used sparingly and almost never in the generic sense.

**Examples:**
- "I've yet to observe evidence of a single 'real world'..."
- "I remember an incredible moment when someone dropped a concise insight..."
- "I desperately hope the optimistic future awaits."
- "I was reading an article about the state of SaaS recently, and came across this line..."
- "I've been thinking about this for a while."

---

## Feature 20: The "both/and" turn — explicit refusal to collapse to one pole

**Rule:** When presenting a tension or polarity, explicitly refuse to collapse to one side. Phrases like "two things can be true," "both are real," and "yes, and also" recur. This is a voice marker, not just a rhetorical move.

**Examples:**
- "With almost anything in product and work, two things can be true..."
- "It's okay to be optimistic and pessimistic at the same time."
- "Both of these people think they're being optimistic. Both of them are probably right. And both of them are probably fooling themselves about something."
- "Both are real optimism, but they protect different things."
- "It has highs and lows these days, with enough inspiring stories to keep me curious and hopeful."

---

## Feature 21: Strategic use of raw quotes from practitioners (usually unnamed)

**Rule:** Embed verbatim quotes from practitioners — often anonymous, sometimes named — in block or inline form. These quotes are always from the messy reality of companies, not from books. They serve as "evidence from the field."

**Examples:**
- "Honestly, it is a shit show. Empowered here means we are basically empowered to negotiate with dozens of teams across massively competing priorities..."
- "Look, at least compared to my last job, this produces reasonably usable software."
- A friend's recent rant over coffee: "I'm not anti-AI. I'm anti how my company is talking about it."
- "Beautiful release notes. But nothing we're shipping is worth writing release notes about."

---

## Feature 22: Repetition of a key phrase for rhetorical hammering

**Rule:** Repeat a central phrase 2–4 times across a piece — sometimes verbatim, sometimes with slight variation — to anchor the reader on a single claim. This is deliberate, not accidental.

**Examples (TBM 417 — Glue People):**
- "And I want to make a passionate plea: before your organization goes off and fires all of its 'glue people' on the assumption that AI can pick up what they were doing, please stop and think..." (repeated nearly verbatim twice)

**TBM 415:**
- "Are you shipping faster than you learn? Or learning faster than you ship?" (appears and then is referenced again multiple times)

**LinkedIn Post (linkedin-c):**
- "Healthy AI skepticism is not negativity. It is one of the ways people show commitment to an outcome." (opens and closes the piece)

---

## Feature 23: The "lo and behold" and "magically, oh magically" irony markers

**Rule:** Use archaic or theatrical expressions ("lo and behold," "magically, oh magically") to signal sarcasm about an obvious or self-defeating outcome. These phrases mark moments where a system produces the opposite of its intended effect.

**Examples:**
- "Lo and behold you are back at being unclear about what is working."
- "And magically, oh magically, those numbers appear out the other side of the spreadsheet."
- "Lo and behold, years later, there is still a checkbox in some system for 'discovery complete'."

---

## Feature 24: "It is very culturally specific" / contextual disclaimer as a closing

**Rule:** After proposing a framework or principle, close with a contextual disclaimer — explicitly noting that the answer depends on culture, context, or organization type. This is not hedging; it is a recurring epistemic commitment to context-dependence.

**Examples:**
- "How to carve out the room to make this happen has no playbook. It is very culturally specific."
- "The calculus here depends on context."
- "There are no one-size-fits-all approaches."
- "There is no formula. There is no framework you can buy."
- "Contextual. Contextual. Contextual."

---

## Feature 25: The "Aside" mid-essay digression

**Rule:** In longer pieces, explicitly label a digression with "Aside," "A quick detour," or a similar marker, then resume the main argument. The aside is always substantive, not cosmetic.

**Examples:**
- "Aside, during this time, the org is intentionally addressing the core architectural issues..." (TBM 402)
- "A Quick Detour (POM)" — full labeled subsection (TBM 402)
- "(Note, we're shifting org examples here.)" (TBM 415)

---

## Feature 26: Structural labeling of the essay's own narrative arc

**Rule:** When the essay follows a deliberate narrative structure (e.g., Act 1/2/3, or a loop diagram), Cutler labels the structure explicitly inside the text. He tells you what the structure is, then runs through it.

**Examples (TBM 402):**
- "Act 1: Delivery Predictability and WIP Reduction At Scale"
- "Act 2: Early Introduction Of Goals"
- (etc. through Act 5)
- "This is not a maturity model. It is a prototypical, yet nuance-filled journey."

**TBM 407:**
- "Strategic juggling / Lazy juggling / Survival juggling" — labeled categories with arrows
- "Virtuous Loop / Vicious Loop / Good → Bad Crossover"

---

## Feature 27: Direct address in second person — "You" accusations and invitations

**Rule:** Shifts suddenly into direct second person to implicate the reader in the dynamic being described. "You" is used both accusatorially ("you end up with...") and invitingly ("ask yourself..."). The effect is urgent and personal, not distant.

**Examples:**
- "You end up with a peanut butter strategy."
- "Ask yourself: were you curious the last time someone challenged one of your deeply held beliefs?"
- "So you want your team to figure out how to use AI for the benefit of your company? Then stop stripping them of the agency to do it."
- "Give me a real problem, give me space to figure out whether AI helps solve it, and get out of my way."

---

## Feature 28: "Not X. Y." sentence pattern (the period-separated turnaround)

**Rule:** Use a two-beat sentence pair — a denial followed by the positive assertion — with a period (not a comma or "but") between them. This creates a hard rhetorical stop before the real claim.

**Examples:**
- "Not a linear journey. A stumbling forward."
- "Not because you doubt the team, but because you trust the team enough to handle the truth."
- "There's no view from nowhere."
- "Stewardship. Thriftiness. Conviction held loosely enough to update but firmly enough to act on."
- "The goal isn't perfect measurement. It's continuously increasing confidence in the core underlying hypotheses."

---

## Feature 29: Newsletter salutation/sign-off convention

**Rule:** TBM newsletters do not use conventional "Dear reader" salutations or "Best, John" sign-offs. They open directly into a personal note or link, and close with a question list — no formal close. The absence of formality is itself the convention.

**Examples:**
- Each TBM opens with either: a tool announcement, a personal reflection, a link, or an apology for a gap in posting.
- Closes are abrupt: the final question or statement is the final sentence. No "warmly" or "thanks for reading."

---

## Feature 30: "The cruelest version of this" / "The deepest version of this" escalation pattern

**Rule:** After establishing a problem, signal the most damaging or subtle variant by escalating with "The cruelest version of this," "The deepest version of this," or "The most consequential version." This moves from general to worst-case without losing analytical tone.

**Examples:**
- "The cruelest version of this: the glue person's work was often invisible because they were good at it."
- "The deepest version of the break is this: even when AI successfully produces the artifact, the constraint shifts rather than dissolves."
- "The most consequential version of the problem."

## Summary of Most Distinctive Voice Features

### Top-tier (most uniquely Cutlerian):

1. **Dialogue staging** (X:/Me: or role-label/Me:) — he voices both sides to avoid strawmanning while still surfacing the naive view.
2. **Essay-closing question lists** — 3–8 numbered or bulleted open questions that replace a conclusion; this is a near-universal pattern in newsletter pieces.
3. **"Wicked cycle" and structural loop labeling** — explicit naming of feedback loops using arrows (→) and loop type labels (Virtuous/Vicious).
4. **"For real." fragments** — short, declarative fragments that follow complex sentences as rhetorical hammers.
5. **Section headers as mini-theses** — headers that argue, not just label ("The Myth of the Standard Framework," "You Can't Cold-Start This").
6. **Parenthetical asides that do real work** — hedges, qualifications, and irony inserted via parentheses or em-dashes, never decorative.
7. **Repetition of a phrase to anchor** — central claim repeated verbatim 2–4 times.

### Strong secondary patterns:

8. Contextual disclaimer closings ("it is very culturally specific")
9. "Not X. Y." turnaround pattern
10. ALL CAPS reserved for genuine emotional peaks (rare and precise)
11. Raw anonymous practitioner quotes as field evidence
12. "Lo and behold" / "magically, oh magically" irony markers
13. Both/and refusal to collapse tensions
14. Ellipsis as trailing ironic signal
15. Aside/detour labeling of mid-essay digressions
