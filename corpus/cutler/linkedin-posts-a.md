## Post 1 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** Prioritization and trade-offs

You can't prioritize something unless you deprioritize something else. Otherwise, it isn't a priority, it's just another task on the list. There's nothing wrong with tasks—they're just not priorities. To avoid this, try this prompt: When faced with the decision of whether to spend time and energy __________ or to invest their time and energy in ______________, we are encouraging the team to prioritize the latter over the former. Example: When faced with the decision of whether to spend time and energy exclusively on acquiring new customers or to invest their time and energy in retaining and deepening relationships with our existing customer base, we are encouraging the team to prioritize the latter over the former.

## Post 2 (date unknown — ~2021)
**Topic:** Product managers and spec writing

A product manager who doesn't write specs/stories/tickets? That's blasphemy say some. That's incredible, say others. IME, you tend to see this practice when the following pieces of the puzzle are in place:

1. Engineers have the time/space to get involved. They aren't drowning under a stream of stories and work.

2. There's less premature convergence -- in the sense that there is less pressure on product managers to "nail down everything" before sharing it with "the team". Short sentences suffice, with the confidence that together the team will be able to run with the it.

3. Engineers are empowered to be more product-centric. Their incentives match that! In the sense that getting involved with "breaking work down" is not seen as something that happens outside the normal "day job". In my experience, in product-forward organizations you will almost always find engineers who would be perfectly great product managers should they want to go in that direction. It isn't required, and many chose not to, but the chops are there due to practice and experience.

4. The team has the room/space to truly start efforts together, vs. a small group going off to nail everything down, and then "presenting it to the team". Being involved from the beginning gives everyone the required context to "write stories".

5. Enough designers! When the design org is short-staffed, there's typically pressure to converge on spec/designs, because it is unclear whether they'll be available to approach things more organically.

6. Direct contact with customers on the part of the whole team. A lot of spec writing is proxying. Proxying information and context from one place, and capturing it in a document. When the team has direct access and context, there is less need for rigid specs.

7. Breaking down the walls of "build the right thing" and "build it right". The team collectively takes ownership of outcomes. When you see this...you tend to get less transactional relationships.

8. Other types of writing! When PMs aren't mired in spec writing, they can focus on gathering data/context about the actual problem and mission. Paradoxically, when this writing is in place, there's less of a need for rigid specs and stories.

9. Letting go of the "ticket" concept altogether. The team might collaboratively evolve a prototype that serves as a "spec". And then work from that.

10. Fewer hoops for product managers to prioritize work. In many cases, the need to "Write Stories" is driven by a need for estimates and approval...vs. a mission driven team tasked with an outcome. IOW, stories and specs have lost their original meaning, and are often more about transactions, "commitments", and perverse measurement schemes. When the opposite is true...you see less of a transactional relationship.

## Post 4 (date unknown — ~2022)
**Topic:** Hustle culture and burnout in product management

Product management hustle culture is burning people out. Self-improvement is great. Don't get me wrong. But people put so much pressure on themselves to absorb every little tip, every tweet, every bit of bubble-gum advice, every case study, and every hack. It's OK to just relax and do your job. It is OK to learn on the job. It is OK to emotionally disengage from your company, and disconnect from the constant grind sometimes. It's OK to laugh at how dysfunctional your company is sometimes, and not care. It's OK to wait things out. You don't need to be "on" all the time.

## Post 6 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** Cadenced product reviews — principles

Some principles for good, cadenced product reviews:

Be present. If you are in the room, be in the room. Leaders coach, facilitate, and guide. The role of a leader during a review is to support the team. You're there to share context, ask good questions, get the information you need to help the team, and provide helpful guidance. This isn't about you.

See the forest AND the trees. When discussing details, remind people of the big picture. When talking about the big picture, remind people of the tangible implications. Don't get stuck in the clouds, and don't get stuck in the weeds.

Keep continuity (but adapt when necessary). A review is a continuation of a dialogue. Close the loop on past questions and action items. Revisit assumptions, and indicate trends. Keep refining your format, and be willing to adapt when necessary. Think of reviews as the beginning and the end of a loop. This is a habit, not a performance.

What?, So What?, Now What? Identify the facts and circumstances surrounding the issue. Explore the implications and significance of the problem. Identify potential paths forward.

Pick data wisely. "If the statistics are boring, you've got the wrong numbers" (Edward R. Tufte). Not all data is good. Powerful ideas imperfectly measured beat perfect measures for not-so-powerful ideas.

Be ready to ask for what you need. A team doesn't need all the answers, but if they need help, they should be prepared to ask for it. Get specific—who, what, when, where, and why.

Don't sugar coat. A review is about what is happening (and what has happened), not what we wish had happened. Foster an environment that is safe for people to surface and discuss challenges.

Prioritize. Direct people's attention to what matters right now. Everything may feel equally important but go in with a force ranking of focus areas. It's your time, and people are there to support you. Make it count.

Don't pitch. Avoid shiny objects and selling. A review is about context, progress, and alignment. Ideally, you have goals and a strategy; if you don't, set aside time to pull those things together.

## Post 7 (date unknown — ~2021)
**Topic:** Breaking the success theater cycle

It can be very hard to break this cycle. There's no sugar-coating the situation. When nothing is really "working" (or when it is hard to understand what is working, or when there's a lot of success theater, and people sense that)...your only option is to go broad, and settle for ineffective proxies. Pressure increases. You're focused on being "responsive", saying yes, the optics of a "solid plan", and focusing on things like predictability and doing what you said you promised to do. You end up with a peanut butter strategy. That in turn negatively impacts outcomes and your ability to measure outcomes. Lo and behold you are back at being unclear about what is working. How do you break out of this? Anyone who tells you "just ___" is fooling themselves. The trick is having something WORK. For real. Success invites success. How to carve out the room to make this happen has no playbook. It is very culturally specific.

## Post 8 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** What is a product manager's job? (dialogue)

X: What is the product manager's job?
Me: A successful product
X: That's so vague
Me: Yeah, what that means in different contexts can vary
X: Do you mean like revenue?
Me: Sustainable, differentiated growth. Making sure the product keeps its promise to your customers. Not short-term revenue. Unless...the only way to survive is to make the quarter.
X: Should I write tickets?
Me: I dunno, is that's what needed at that company to have a successful product? There are probably higher leverage things to do, but...
X: Discovery?
Me: Discovering what?
X: Product discovery?
Me: Depends on what the research methods used, and goal of the research. Research—including participatory research—is a skill. You can learn all that, or hire for it.
X: Strategy?
Me: Yeah. But make sure it helps people focus instead of just look good on a slide.
X: But success is so....vague
Me: Yeah, that's the job. In a sense, framing success to begin with is a big part of being a PM. And getting people on board with that definition
X: Does this mean you should tell people what to do?
Me: Maybe. But when is that ever the best way to make progress? You end up with people who build anything to avoid having to talk to you.
X: But what if the company doesn't believe in this? They just want to give you features to build?
Me: Well, they think they know better about what it takes to have a successful product. Do they? If so, fine. If not, that's a problem.
X: But what if the engineers aren't building? Or designers not designing? Am I responsible for that? You need those things to have a successful product.
Me: If it is fixable, maybe try. But if it isn't, then that is a constraint you'll need to work around. Also...make sure you aren't making things worse. That's often the case.
X: I'm burnt out trying to be all things to my company. There's so much needed for a successful product, and it all falls on me.
Me: That's hard. And it is a privilege to just be able to up and leave. What is within your control that will set you up to have another shot at a successful product at your next company?
X: Hmmm. Shouldn't engineers and designers care about a successful product as well?
Me: Ideally, but there are a lot of wonderfully engineered and amazingly designed products that never survive. That's where you come in.
X: What about if my product is internal?
Me: Still...successful product. Who are you serving? What is success? Product management is highly contextual. At the end of the day, "successful product" will suffice as the goal.

## Post 9 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** Product strategy vs. grand plans

I really like this post by Itamar Gilad. It captures some things on my mind lately. It feels as if many teams conflate strategy with a "Grand Plan". They work backwards from a grand vision (or a grand revenue goal) and ask "what will get in the way" and "what obstacles must we overcome to get there". Instead, my belief is that strategy is about cultivating conditions where luck combined with momentum/inertia will work in your favor. It creates more optionality, remixing of ideas, and new/emergent opportunities.

## Post 10 (date unknown — ~2022)
**Topic:** Run, run, run — against crawl/walk/run frameworks

Instead of crawl, walk, run ... try run, run, run. There's no use in crawling, if your goal is running. Similarly, with product, don't sequester teams away from customers and outcomes, and have them focus on "delivery" as a form of crawling. Encourage them to work their way around this loop—a minimal strategy, a basic model, prioritize opportunities, design bets/experiments, measure impact, and integrate learning. Sure, at first things will be rough and minimal. You will not do fancy research, or A/B tests, or big complex releases. Things will go wrong. But you'll start doing "the thing" instead of a perversion of the thing. Does this sound scary? Customer contact? Experiments? Outcomes? Then it is leadership that is learning how to crawl, walk, run ... not the teams. Two related posts in the comments.

## Post 11 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** Product team predictability — what works and what doesn't

How to become "more predictable" as a product team:

What TO do:
* Work smaller
* Work together
* Work on less at once
* Start finishing, stop starting
* Regularly integrate your work
* Feedback early and often
* Diverge at first (let design emerge)
* Emphasize workflow, adapt scope to time
* Continuous improvement and learning
* Support teams with proper systems and tools
* Recognize accomplishments, schedule downtime
* Visualize workflow and identify bottlenecks
* Recognize predictability as an outcome, not the primary objective

What NOT to do:
* Large batches and siloed work
* Over-schedule quarters with excessive commitments
* Start new initiatives when blocked
* Delay integration of parallel work
* Restrict collaboration on tasks
* Rush toward fixed plans
* Lock designs prematurely
* Invest excessive effort in estimation
* Pressure commitments to specific dates
* Treat projects as isolated endeavors
* Ship without reflection
* Calendar-based work visualization
* Make predictability itself the ultimate goal

## Post 12 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** ProductOps and the cost of consistency

Two very important questions for anyone in ProductOps:
1. "What is the cost of consistency?"
2. "If consistency is desired, how do we make it cheaper and less risky?"

Consistency is never free, and in some cases, it robs teams of their ability to innovate. ProductOps frequently positions itself as operating in the consistency business, prioritizing scaled solutions. However, if the actual goal is organizational effectiveness, teams must evaluate: the downsides of proposed standardization, and methods to minimize those downsides. Rather than pursuing consistency as an end goal, ProductOps should critically examine whether standardization truly delivers value.

## Post 15 (date unknown — ~2023)
**Topic:** Cross-functional empathy — product teams vs. support/marketing

Product is hard, but most product teams don't appreciate how good they have it compared to groups and individuals in customer success, support, and marketing.

Engineer: "Ugh, I can't believe how long that research read-out took. Two hours!"
Customer Success: "Wait, you were able to spend two whole hours learning about our customers?"

Product Manager: "I wish we could experiment more!"
Support: "Wait, you get to experiment?"

Designer: "I only got 2-3hrs of focus time today!"
Marketing: "Focus time?"

Keep this in mind when you get frustrated while working with your extended team.

## Post 16 (~2025, from recent activity)
**Topic:** AI adoption and employee agency

[From TBM 425 / LinkedIn share] So you want your team to figure out how to use AI for the benefit of your company? Then stop stripping them of the agency to do it.

AI gets sold to PMs two ways — one frees people by eliminating status-deck busywork, the other surveils by auto-capturing every status change. The same tool produces opposite outcomes depending on the trust established.

Companies systematically destroy conditions for genuine adoption, then label resistance a mindset problem — when employees are asking entirely rational questions about value, timeline, and who actually benefits. Years of layoffs and broken promises have eroded trust, making employees rightfully skeptical of "this will be great for you" messaging.

What's being destroyed when mandates eliminate choice:
* Autonomy: Mandates eliminate choice
* Competence: AI devalues existing expertise while demanding new skills on company timelines
* Relatedness: Individual "superpower" narratives undermine team collaboration
