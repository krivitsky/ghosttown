at last year's LeSS conf in amsterdam, shared the trend of simpler design
problems being taken over by learning machines (UI design, etc), and by
extrapolation the automation of more and more routine design tasks, and
hence the increasing need for developers that are focused on what is most
hard to automate: tasks that require learning and adaptiveness. ie
"programming as the last job standing" (and "programming" will move ever up
the abstraction stack towards eg creating LMs, etc)

as an impressive example of this, google recently reported research on
using a LM to design a computer chip (their next gen TPU). it is now into
production, and better than anything a human could design -- and prior,
human chip designers scoffed that this was possible.

https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03544-w

now curious to check out github's new copilot

there's a generalizing trend of copilot and the TPU chip designer:
synergistic systems, in which LMs and humans collaborate to design complex
things, which again will focus on using developers for the harder parts:
learning, creative leaps, adapting (and slowly eliminating single-task
specialists)
