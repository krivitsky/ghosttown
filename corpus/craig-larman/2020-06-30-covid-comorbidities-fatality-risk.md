yes-ish. one caveat is that italy data may not closely reflect other
populations -- as they have a very high senior pop, and much data was
collected before latest treatment protocols -- but predict that these
*relative* risk ratios are salient to many populations, since studies
consistently show -- and have tracked a lot, reading the JAMA & NEJM
research reports each week published -- there is relatively low risks <50
if healthy, and a much higher "step change" risk in the 60+ pop

there is also a strong fatality skewing towards those with a comorbidity,
including hypertension (#1), obesity (#2), diabetes (#3), kidney diseases,
CVD, compromised immune system, smoking tobacco, etc. ... it isn't "60+" by
itself per se; it is that 60+ has much higher prevalence of these problems

for example, in a JAMA research report
<https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2765184> of nyc 5700
covid cases and comorbidity: hypertension 57%, obesity 42%, and diabetes
34%. *almost everyone (median age 63) had a comorbidity*. that's a
repeating pattern. the CDC knows this now and has put out a warning
focusing on those with a comorbidity
<https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/need-extra-precautions/people-with-medical-conditions.html?CDC_AA_refVal=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.cdc.gov%2Fcoronavirus%2F2019-ncov%2Fneed-extra-precautions%2Fgroups-at-higher-risk.html>

if u track global health, u know that hypertension, obesity, and type-2
diabetes are "pandemics", so this is a highly influential factor in fatality

the upshot being that the data show that if one is <65 and in excellent
health, the IFR in that sub-pop is thankfully low

all that said, sars-cov-2 can be challenging for even the young & healthy:
my 29 yo fit daughter caught it and was knocked over for a few weeks

on the treatment side, there is also now a lowering of the CFR, as
dexamethasone + remdesivr + proning + avoidingVentilator (i.e.
clarification of protocols that actually work) become widespread
