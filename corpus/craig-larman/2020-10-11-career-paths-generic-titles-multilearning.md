hi alan, and thx for asking.

the policy at AAA (and JP Morgan IIRC) reflects the book-1 experiment in
the Org chapter: *Generic title with levels*. which, interestingly, was
common when i started sw work in canada in the late 70s. e.g.
Product Developer 1 (not SW dev, *product* dev): 1 strong skill
PD 2: 2 strong diff skills
PD 3: 3 strong diff skills
PD 4: 4 strong diff skills, and coach of those
etc. (above is just an example)

tis not an unusual idea -- to create a career path and comp that signals
what is wanted in the behavior. in this case, multi-skill and
multi-learning and coaching

on your related "what to do with middle managers" question: you wrote that
the senior mgr was concerned about them being "just" a team member, and you
suggested Traveler. however having worked with many such, my observation is
that few are deeply skillful in modern sw dev & tech excellence, and
therefore rarely useful at tech coaching, which is the point of Traveler.
thus what is usually revealed is that they don't have much to contribute --
yet, before doing & learning -- at gemba (creating code, sound, graphics,
etc)... and so are rarely motivated as a Traveler, which is for coaching
hands-on expertise of limited skills by strong up-to-date experts. and in
any event, the Traveler guide is not normally meant as permanent, but more
often as a short-term mechanism to deal with some limited knowledge that
needs to be spread, after which the Traveler goes home to 1 team and stops
traveling. further, the Traveler does NOT simply join a team for a sprint;
they have to be invited in. suppose there are putative Traveler
ex-middle-mgrs at Sprint Planning that the teams know don't have much to
concretely offer; how likely are they to invite them in for coaching? all
of this means that Traveler is definitely not the go-to idea for what to
do with extra mgrs. and OTOH your original advice -- become a team member
-- is spot on to the reality of what could actually be useful. living with
the salary inconsistency is probably the least bad option

widening the picture: as so often, scrum/LeSS exposes weaknesses: in this
case, likely that most of the middle mgrs don't have much to contribute --
yet -- at gemba. the senior mgr perhaps doesn't know this, and is in a
fantasy about what they can do and contribute. in my experience "playing
along" with business fantasies rarely ends well ;)
