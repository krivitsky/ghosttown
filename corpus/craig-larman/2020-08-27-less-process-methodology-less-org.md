mary and & tom's recently shared post highlights a pervasive and serious
misunderstanding. it seems widely believed by people who work as just
teachers or small-scale 'agile' (or lean) coaches, without long and deep
experience in large-scale cases...

the widespread use of the terms *process* or *methodology* is meant by many
-- including in this post -- that this is a *behavior change* or a *change
in WoW*. that's a profound misunderstanding of what we are suggesting in
LeSS. if someone were to incorrectly describe LeSS as just a process or
method, we've failed in explaining

and of course if someone were to incorrectly believe or describe LeSS as an
m/p, then what follows from that is a misunderstanding of the change impact
and who needs to be involved in it. e.g. "*LeSS is a method/process. New
methods and processes are things we the senior mgmt (and HR and Finance)
don't need to get involved in, and it would be incorrect micro-mgmt to get
involved in. so of course we support the workers to change their processes
are they see fit; it isn't our business to get involved*."

no. LeSS implies deep structural changes (and policy changes). LeSS is much
better described and understood as a very different *org design*, with the
dissolution and re-creation of new departments, sites, groups, teams,
career paths, roles & resp, and mgr positions... and the related policies
in Finance, HR, etc that are implied

that is so different than a "new method". and because it is deep org design
change, it must involve very senior mgmt in a very serious way at some
point, since they have the levers of control over relevant elements such as
elimination of existing groups, sites, roles, positions, policies, etc that
are implied

that article, and so many others like it, completely misses the point of
what are are suggesting in large-scale change and descaling with LeSS
