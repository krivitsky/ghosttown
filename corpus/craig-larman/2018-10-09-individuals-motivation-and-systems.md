josef,


hi and thx for this question, which felt worth its own thread


first, large-scale scrum is scrum. so we are not focusing on repeating all the basic important elements of scrum and the agile values, including the importance of individuals, motivation, etc. we are focusing on the deltas more or things that are not emphasized enough in basic scrum but that are necessary for success in large product groups. otherwise we end of repeating everything important that's already been covered in foundational material and foundational key points


that said, note that in book 1 (which lays out the deeper points of LeSS that we are suggesting) in both the *Feature Teams* and *Organization* chapters (and perhaps *Teams* chapter iirc), we highlight many times the individuals and intrinsic motivation story, and *especially connect it to org design elements*


that last point is important, and is related to "culture follows structure". for example, if you have single-function groups (e.g. an architecture group, ...) that are not directly connected to the real users and customers, and not intimately part of the feedback loop of delivery and customer experience, then this leads to a systemic environment where people will usually care less about "the whole" and the customer. or, the org has offshore "low cost" sites where the developers are treated like "resources" to dig holes, etc.


*have often observed that it is difficult to cultivate intrinsic motivation in employees, but it is very very easy to destroy it*, usually by org design elements such as silo groups, local optimization goals, micro-mgmt, rewards & punishments, etc.


*intrinsic motivation and other behavioral/attitudinal elements are strongly influenced by the system*. in english math phraseology one uses the phrase in theorems, lemmas, and proofs, "...a necessary but not sufficient precondition...". in other words, it is vital as a "necessary but not sufficient" first step to remove those org elements that kills the chance for caring about the customer and intrinsic motivation, and secondly, set up a system where there is the *invitation and space* for it to arise. this is not the whole job, but a critical part where org design is salient


am sure many of us have seen or been in situations where we strove to inculcate intrinsic motivation and caring about the customer and focus on improving the individual or team spirit etc, or have seen places where there are slogans for variations of this ("we care about quality!"), but... the org system itself was set up to fight or degrade this attempt. have seen many coaches who are not yet thinking/seeing as a systems thinker and naively assume that they can fix a team locally, not realizing that there are large forces in the way that really need to be dealt with if we want to create a sustainable environment in which the seeds of motivation are nourished rather than poisoned


in a way, all this boils down to systems thinking: recognizing that the individual elements are *strongly* influenced by interaction effects and the system, and that trying to locally improve individuals and teams in an org design that is stacked against them, is a bit of pissing in the wind


so would like to suggest that if we want to fix the root causes preventing intrinsic motivation and really achieve it sustainably in a large product group, there needs to (1) first be a focus on fixing the systemic org design inhibitory elements, and then in the *inviting space*, (2) coach locally the individuals


of course we recognize the importance of (2) and assume that's all part of understanding "large-scale scrum is scrum" and the role of SM, etc. the LeSS-specific suggestions -- which are mostly systemic suggestions -- we are focusing on (1) to provide the space for successful (2)


hope that helps clarify why we are not emphasizing basic scrum/agile values and the importance of motivated individuals
