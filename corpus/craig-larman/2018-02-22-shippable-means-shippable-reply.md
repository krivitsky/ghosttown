hi. that's clear and appreciate it. minor thoughts:

if not already, suggest reading the "Product" chapter in book 3 so there's
understanding of what we mean by shippable product increment

on part/component/feature definitions, in LeSS guess could say that the
focus is on *integration* (and its consequences) over ... how to put it ...
the ontology of separate elements.

aside:, in robotics, a common language framing is "*skills*". it is true
that a self-driving car has a few large elements that a human might call
"features", such as "drive yourself", but the language of "features"
doesn't feel quite right below "drive yourself". is "change lane by
yourself" a "feature"? of course one can call it that, but it's a bit
forced. but in the language of robotics one can speak of the car robot
having *skills* such as "change lane", etc. i suspect this language framing
will become increasingly relevant in LeSS, in domains creating autonomous
intelligent agents

aside aside: ... and with the machine *learning* side becoming almost
trivial with the rapid maturation of ML tool chains, i predict there will
be a shift to a strong focus on improving techniques & tech for *machine
teaching*... and mostly teaching *skills, rather than "features"*
