wanted to highlight this response to john's question on the "communist"
thread, because *False Dichotomies* is a chapter in the *Thinking Tools*
section of book 1 very deliberately, for LeSS adopters to take note of...

these questions (from the org folks, not john) reminds me of the chapter
and experiments in book 1, *False Dichotomies*

see variations of this *so* often, that's why we wrote that chapter in the
thinking tools section, to suggest to LeSS adopters to add this to their
mental models pattern matching as a major repeating thinking
(non-thinking?) pattern, which u can work with at that level of debugging

the actual experiment is "*Try...Identify and avoid false dichotomies*"

for example, using this thinking tool of "*Identify and avoid false
dichotomies*", when one hears the phrase ...

"*everyone is the same*", that can be seen as the formulation, "*if people
aren't single specialists, then they are all the same*". or simplified,
"single-specialists vs generalists"

as though there was no third (or tenth) alternative

to help people own this insight, a suggestion is to start simply in a LeSS
adoption coaching with introducing the concept of false dichotomies in a
kind of socratic approach, and asking people for examples. make a visible
list. ask them to explore with each other why this thinking pattern happens

then when the specific argument arises of *everyone is the same* then comes
the delicate part: *how to help this person see this is a false-dichotomy
framing*? without becoming defensive? there are many ways and it is bound
to in part depend on the person, but suggest that a generalization is to
find a way that they can own the insight. and if you have previously during
group teaching gone through recognizing false dichotomy framing, it will be
easier.

want to stress again that added that chapter because tis a *major* pattern
in LeSS adoptions, and it is useful to proactively ameliorate it with
upfront education/awareness

of course, the question can be also answered at the "surface" level of what
multi-skilled workers really means, primary, secondary, tertiary skill
differences, differential salary for people with more skills and more
initiative, etc etc etc, but suggest instead to start at the "pattern
level" e.g. "could that be a false dichotomy framing?" because if we can
help people in an org to acquire these Thinking Tools for themselves, then
the detailed issues are easier to work thru
