have helped in 20-ish value-stream analysis (perhaps better called *WASTE*-stream analysis) workshops over the yrs, and by far the best "tool" have experienced is a gigantic room, or hallways, in which one can use post-its to linearly lay out -- across the span of the walls -- all the hour-by-hour analysis of the human activities in the work stream, and then classify as value-add (eg creating code that the customer actually wants & will use) vs waste (most everything else). mary & tom's notation covered in LSD 1 (iirc) is good 'nuff. both the ease of use in this approach in a group, and ease of seeing the whole, is unparalleled.

a few tips from my experience (which may of course be incorrect):
* the work steam analysis should be "concept to cash"

* if one does the analysis truly from "concept to cash", was very rare to see less than 95% *waste* in the work stream (ie 5% value-add in the work stream), for traditional org designs of product dev. so be suspicious if it's much diff in yr cases

* it is common to discover that 3/4 of the total waste in the work stream *precedes* the "concept" even getting to R&D. it is most often in upfront groups such as product mgmt, sales, analysis groups, etc.

* related to last 2 points, as a result, doing this exposes and provokes *political* responses, related to larman's laws, such as "it can't possibly be true that our analysis group is doing just waste", etc. then people start to play language/word redefinition games (law 2) such as calling non-paying-customers as "internal customers" and the related wastes as "value". eg "we are the DB access layer component group; our customer is the middle-layer component group, and everything we do/create is value for them." or "we are the UI design group, and our customer -- for our documents -- is the downstream front-end dev group, and we do/create is value for them." ad nauseum. then everything that is actually waste is redefined as "value", and we're off to the races of dysfunction, and not improving the system.

on your azure devops question, sorry, now confused. how is doing a VSM analysis in a workshop related to such tools? as an aside like to suggest, "we model to have a conversation", so that the people in the workshop develop shared understanding, and personal insight, and especially for something like a vsm workshop, using computer tools really gets in the way of all the people who should be there (eg senior mgmt) *owning* the insights from themselves doing the analysis and learning/talking together, while creating the visualization together on the walls.
