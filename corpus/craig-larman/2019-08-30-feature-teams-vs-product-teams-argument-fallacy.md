would be easy to go to the lower level of the specific misunderstandings and assumptions in this article, but perhaps a more educational point is to see that this is an example of the -- very common -- argument fallacy called "argumentum ad dictionarium" or "appeal to definition", which is usually a subtype of the argument fallacy called "argument by false premises" which starts with a false definition and then is off to the races

as a trainer or coach, it is useful to be able to quickly classify arguments into their logical fallacy categories. why? so can move to the meta-level of the argument fallacy, from which most of the noise of the details at the "instance level" is irrelevant, the pattern is revealed, and you can more quickly explore or dispense with the issue at the pattern level

probably an interesting new thread on this list would be: what are the most common categories of argument fallacies or logical fallacies that you encounter? how do you deal with them?

note that instances of the False Dichotomy argument fallacy are so commonly heard in LeSS adoptions that we elevated that to one of the key thinking tools in book 1
