== on Adaptiveness and "Value"

first: don't make a self-licking ice-cream cone

i.e. tis true that the org design from LeSS is consistent with broad adaptiveness, but... suggest that we coach that adaptiveness is not for its own sake. rather, adaptiveness is in the service of something else: learning towards the discovery and delivery of high value or high impact, in the context of a world in which it's usually difficult to a priori know for sure what is high value/impact, and a world in which competition does stuff, new technologies emerge, new trends emerge, etc.

i.e. making cheap and easy the ability to learn and then also to change to new directions, in the service of something else... usually high value/impact.

this is a "why?" message that was around in the early days of xp/scrum/ASD

this connects to martin's snowbird post on the choice of the name "agile":
"We considered a bunch of names, and agreed eventually on 'agile' as we felt that captured the adaptiveness and response to change which we felt was so important to our approach."

it isn't that am thinking that we are optimizing for definite highest value -- although that would be nice -- because that is a speculation (usually) and things change, so would not say that LeSS can optimize for (1) definite highest value and (2) adaptiveness. rather, will say that we are aiming to increase adaptiveness & learning... in the service of more probably discovering and delivering high value

of course, having 1 (and only 1) re-prioritized PB shared amongst many teams, structurally (in the org design) increases the chance that all teams are more likely delivering higher value from global view. so with that org design element (1 PB) we see an example of directly aspiring to optimize for delivery of high value. but once again, since the priorities in the list are hypotheses rather than truths, what is actually delivered may or may not be the most valuable. BUT, with adaptiveness and learning as the overarching capability, there's a greater chance that each Sprint will be delivering high value/impact

in short, if have explained this reasonably well, one should be able to see that i distinguish between the following 2 statements:
* (how i would say it) LeSS is for broad adaptiveness in the service of learning and delivering high value/impact
* LeSS is for broad adaptiveness and high value

== on a goal for an org design

directionally (increasing/decreasing) one can influence an organizational design with respect to certain goals or objectives. for example, if...

(a) there are many component teams with lists of component tasks, and functional teams with lists of functional tasks drawn from many small "products", VS
(b) feature teams with 1 PB with 1 broad product def

... then with (b) it is possible to show (and observe) that each "sprint" there is a greater chance that from the global view that teams are delivering items of higher value (and also that broad adaptiveness is higher)

etc

there are a set of influential org design elements that influence this goal: the size of the product def, the number of lists, the nature of teams

picking up on bas's comment, "Getting an agreement on it [a goal for the org design] can help a lot in determining the direction of the improvements." indeed. like a perfection vision. that's a key point of why it's useful to have some vision or objective for the org design. for example, suppose that "Igor" says, "i want component teams and small products!" there's no platonic good or bad in Igor's desire... whether his idea for the org design is "good" or "bad" depends on some goal or vision. if it was Secrecy, his recommendation would be consistent with the desired direction of improvement. OTOH if it was "global adaptiveness in the service of learning & delivering high value", it would not be directionally consistent.

pls don't think that am suggesting that one can deterministically design an org system and 100% deterministically realize some goal. that would be simplified False Dichotomy thinking. but there are definitely org design elements (such as the size of product def and nature of teams) that are influential, directionally
