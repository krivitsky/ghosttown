some of u already know that one of the major problems in biology (or
biochem/biophysics) is the protein-folding problem. proteins are key to bio
processes and it is their folded shape which is key to their function. the
breakthrough of solving it begins an entire new era in
medicine/biochem/etc. it's been a central unsolved problem for the 50+
years it's been known, and thousands of PhDs and entire research careers
have been dedicated to it. there's even an bi-annual competition for
research teams to make some minor incremental improvement. it intersects
with computer science, as supercomputers and complex computational models
are created to explore the problem.

*last year, deepmind (the google group in london at the heart of most of
their AI advances) solved protein folding*. this is a breakthrough event in
biochem and computational biology.
https://deepmind.com/blog/article/alphafold-a-solution-to-a-50-year-old-grand-challenge-in-biology

but the key point wish to highlight is how it was solved. not with
thinking, not with lovely math models, not with coding, but "simply" with
deep-learning machines that learned about the problem and "solved" it with
a complex neural net

this AlphaFold solution (along with AlphaGo) highlights an important
milestone shift in dev methods (along with how the Tesla and Waymo Drivers
are learning to drive): solutions from learning machines rather than
imperative coding.

this leads to an emerging movement: software [development] 2.0, in which
learning machines are used, in whole or in part in synergy with
traditional sw [dev] 1.0 coding.
https://karpathy.medium.com/software-2-0-a64152b37c35

this raises the bar once again on the necessary education and skill of
developers: just as software dev 1.0 is eating the world, software dev 2.0
will eat into software dev 1.0, and increasingly devs will need mastery of
both. predict that sw 2.0 won't supplant sw 1.0 as there are many problems
for which imperative coding is far more straightforward (e.g. "red light
means stop" is simply and clearly solved with traditional logic), but sw
2.0 will need to enter the toolkit of more and more devs

as common in emerging ideas/tech it starts with single specialists ("AI"
and "data science" people). but google especially, and others, are racing
to simplify and democratize the tools & ideas space so that normal
multi-learning devs can pick up and master this subject (e.g. google is
working on LMs that themselves create LMs). hence -- connecting this to
LeSS and org designs optimized for adaptiveness -- the predictable
local-optimization thinking mistake made by org-design-naive people of "for
this specialization we need a single-specialization team" (the AI team, the
data science team) will of course be promoted and defended ala larman's
laws for some time, but eventually it will start to be seen as an
unnecessary complexification as it becomes easier to apply LMs -- which is
becoming true even today. thus suggest to not lock in to the
local-optimization of yet another specialist group, at least for groups
just doing application of LMs (vs specialist groups still doing hard
research for LMs).
