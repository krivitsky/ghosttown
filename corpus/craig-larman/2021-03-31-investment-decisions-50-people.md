in LeSS in book 3 we cover the 3 adoption principles, and give some explanations. "deep & narrow" is indeed for a max of around 50 people. this is an empirical observation -- having been involved in quite a few adoptions over many years. in part it's related to the amount of "debts" that have to be paid down when making such a deep change: learning debts, behavior/mindset debts, tech debts. and also due to the large and quite diff set of new skills that are needed amongst the devs and others; e.g. how to effectively work in shared code with TDD, integrating continuously, learning all the skills of inter-team coordination (from effective communities to multi-team design workshops to participatory decision making) in a self-managing org all without managers involved. and so much more. it's a *big* set of difficult changes, and coaches who have been involved in something close to a LeSS adoption will quickly attest to how hard and large the learning required is, and how many ways it can fall apart, and ... that it's strongly aggravated "linearly" by more and more people involved. hence "50" max

and as gene mentioned, it also means the investment and risk is well bounded... it won't blow up a large existing org

and because a successful smooth-ish LeSS adoption benefits from *before* sprint 1 -- this is the "deep" part of deep and narrow -- in the new org design a set of formal OD changes: formally eliminating the existing mgmt positions, eliminating existing teams, eliminating existing job titles, eliminating existing financial & decision-making processes, and even more, and then formally creating new job titles & career paths, new teams, and so much more... all that is very difficult to set up and realize in a large group all at once. further a LeSS adoption needs only *volunteers* who are truly educated/informed of these major changes in their career and how will be working, and who then eagerly still want to do this -- all in the context of having grown up in the traditional org with a set of expectations of what they are doing and where going -- thus it is not the case that there will be a long lineup to volunteer for this significant formal change in their work lives. so again, "50"

i don't know of any large company that's done a complete adoption, though there may be some in eastern europe (where LeSS adoptions seem more prevalent due to less prior influence of PMI, etc). we tend to work in hw/sw product companies (e.g. an ericsson, bmw, nokia net, etc) where each product group is basically independent of others, so that the vast majority of these big companies in other product groups remain in their traditional "customized agile" model, and only one of the product groups makes a change, usually due to some real sense of urgency. e.g. the bmw self-driving car group

oh... over the 40+ years involved in sw dev and change fads, have not seen a single large-scale case where a positive deep change lasted more than about 5 years. the key reason seems to be the turnover of the senior mgmt. the new incoming inevitably is lost in local-optimization cognitive biases (not systems thinkers), not insightful about the real work or skillful org design for some optimizing goal, and also... has to introduce yet another change because of a set of dysfunctional forces in large orgs.
