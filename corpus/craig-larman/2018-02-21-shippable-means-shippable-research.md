hubert,

first, thank you for asking

leaving aside your alluded ken's point that perhaps he made to you that
"shippable means really shippable each sprint", it seems to me that your
framing/advice below is "reasonably logical" and would be reasonably
applied by some kinda equivalent of the "homo economicus
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_economicus>" archetype in classical
economics -- in process land as "*homo processicus*". but critically as you
probably know, tversky, kahneman, and thaler (all recent nobel laureates)
have shown that "homo economicus" is not how people actually behave/decide,
due to evolutionary psychological and social psych forces

in short, i observe that it often boils down to variations of larman's laws
<http://larmanslaws.org/>. how you reasonably frame/advise things below is *easily
taken as raw meat for the forces of status quo* in org structure and roles,
by law #1, and then applied in laws 2 & 3. am quite sure you mean your
framing in the best and wisest sense, perhaps assuming *homo processicus*.
i don't work so much in teaching (where it is very easy to miss really
seeing & getting how this dynamic plays out), but more often in an org
adopting LeSS. and observe over time what actually happens: that managers
and single-specialists and their related formal groups will fight really
hard to maintain the status quo of org structure, and will use anything
they can to defend that stasis. and the framing/guidance you give below is
trivially easily used to continue justifying component teams, research
teams, specification teams, HI teams, BA teams, UX teams, tool teams, ML
teams, and other silos ad nauseum, ... as they "produce knowledge" ;)

so have learned over the years -- as has ken -- that one must be rather
unequivocal on this point, to realistically deal with the status quo forces
& desires, which are *huge*.

note that law #3 explicitly points out how people use the "pragmatic"
euphemism to maintain the status quo

so in LeSS we are very clear on the result of a sprint, and this is in part
to address the the forces of larman's laws, recognizing that *homo
processicus* is a fantasy. that -- as ken means as well -- shippable means
really shippable (a truly done to-the-market product) each sprint. so the LeSS
rules <https://less.works/less/rules/index.html> contain phrases such as:

*[the sprint] it results in a shippable product each Sprint*
*Each Sprint results in an integrated whole product*

... and that means truly shippable to the market, every sprint, from sprint
1

now, in pure SW, in the "less than 8 teams" case, this is almost trivial to
achieve... and btw if there is a pure-sw product org that can't do this
from sprint 1 in a smaller LeSS fw adoption, there's almost always
something funny goin' on, and it is probably a "bottom up" change attempt,
rather than the necessary LeSS requirement of top-down & bottom up adoption
support

but in hw/sw and LeSS Huge cases, we recognize this is harder at the start,
and so LeSS includes incremental adoption concepts such as expanding
definition of done, FTAMs, etc.

but we never stray from the "true north" vision that the output of a sprint
should be a shippable product, not just "more knowledge"

this doesn't mean, btw, that there is no place for research activities in
LeSS. we have written about research in LeSS in both book 2 (in the
Requirements chapters) and in book 3 (in the Product Backlog chapter). but
the research is done in the context of or parallel with creating something
concrete and shippable, even though of course it may not be minimally
useful yet. btw, i observe that most "research" in product dev is not
research in the scientific university sense, but more often *reading,
choosing, evaluating, and experimenting with alternatives from a set of
algorithms/hw components/FOSS sw components/etc*. and it seems to me that
it is often called "research" by people in the group who have come in from
a masters or phd, and want to continue *calling* their work "research"
since it feels more important to them then -- even though it is not
research in the scientific sense, or by people in some kinda career track
from the "dev group" to the "research group" which view this as a social
promotion, when in fact what they doing is... *reading, choosing,
evaluating, and experimenting with alternatives from a set of algorithms/hw
components/FOSS sw components/etc*.... something that a regular feature
team in LeSS can and should do, perhaps by applying the *Leading Team*
guide approach.
