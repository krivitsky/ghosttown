NB! it is extremely unlikely to really have sustained and adaptive feature teams without first changing the job title and career path model, and formally having volunteers move to the new "broad" job title (e.g. Prod Dev) with a consistent career path based on multi-learning & multi-skills. an adaptive feature team is based on adaptive feature-team members. and this takes us to "culture follows structure." to wit: it's a fantasy to think that if someone has an official job title of BA, and in 4 weeks have a review to go from BA-1 t BA-2 with more salary, then it's a fantasy to say to them, "hey, ignore your official job title, career path, and measurement system; instead, focus on learning different skills such as dev and HI design." etc. ditto for every other specialist role. it's HR 101: the title, path, and rewards need to be consistent with the behaviors desired. so not until a set of volunteers has formally shifted to the new job title & path will one see a sustained behavior of multi-learning and adaptiveness. another reason for Parallel Org.

that's one of myriad examples that one can't just adopt "some parts." it simply doesn't create the change desired. this isn't about me sharing a perfection vision; have seen, and other coaches have seen, and Galbraith and Kotter and other change researchers have seen, that the expected changes don't actually happen -- and then the benefit doesn't come -- if the org design is inconsistent with the *espoused* changes of behavior. it's a *system*
