also watch out for the False Dichotomy framing: either a person is an
expert/specialist in *only* *one* thing, or they are not an
expert/specialist. of course that is silly, but is a super common sloppy
thinking mistake

the framing that i suggest to people is to first all recognize that a
person can be or become an expert in 2+ things over time (and in fact this
is quite common), and to think of people has having *primary*, *secondary*,
*tertiary* specialities, etc

i often say, "sure we want specialists! but don't conflate *specialist*
with *single specialist*." many people can become specialists in 2+ skills,
given the time & support.

this last point is salient: many managers especially have had the
experience -- if they have any experience at all in developing -- of
quickly clawing their way up some career path into mgr positions from dev
positions. e.g. maybe a dev for only "8" years... if that. (in india, it's
like 4 years). so that's part of the mis-framing problem: they project
their prior experience on to the situation of what people can become. BUT
if a person can remain as a hands-on dev for *30* years (and get paid well
for that) and has the support to learn in new areas, and (culture follows
structure) there is a career path of general title *Product*Developer-1 to
*Product*Developer-5 (not BA-1 to BA-2) where higher levels (and higher
pay) come from being an expert in 2+ skills, then there is the time and
support in place for people to develop multiple expertise

btw, an often unexamined point in this entire conversation is this: *why is
it so fucking hard to learn to work well in component X*? in my decades
working on tons of legacy code in components created by "expert" component
teams, it is super rare that the reason is some *intrinsic* complexity.
rather it is a self-inflicted wound of artificial complexity that makes it
hard to learn and work in a component... and that artificial complexity
ironically comes from the very fact that they have single-component devs!
we cover this dynamic in the "Feature Teams" chapter of book-1, where we
explore how component teams end up creating mediocre, overly-complex code
in their own component, and component team devs rarely rise above the level
of ability to create mediocre code (compared to what is possible if one
understands Clean Code). this artificially created hard effort to work well
in novel components also comes from the lack of TDD & clean code, whose
problem also traces back to mgmt failures

> Hi,

> Just want to share about routing vs adaptive expertise since it is very
> relevant in LeSS adoptions and I only discovered recently:
>
> It is common to get question or remark regarding people developing their
> expertise; usually from managers.
>
> The thinking is e.g.: in case of component teams, members are or will
> become experts of that component. They are hired for that and they might
> leave company if they need to also do something else.
>
> In feature teams, the scope of knowledge and concern is wider and spread
> across multiple technologies and/or components. Therefore, the thinking is
> that people will not develop deep expertise in one specific technology or
> component.
>
> That thinking is pretty much correct. It is called *routine expertise*.
> Like sushi chef which makes perfect always same types of sushi.
>
> Unfortunately, the product development is not like sushi. Everything is
> constantly changing or should be changing. Hence, routine expertise is not
> only not really useful, but it is likely to be a major impediment for e.g.
> architectural improvement.
>
> Does this mean that expertise in general is a bad concept? No, definitely
> not. Also, better avoid telling manager that there are no experts in
> feature teams.
> But, one should value adaptive experts more (
> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_expertise) instead. It is also not
> a matter of choice. Adaptive type of expertise doesn’t contradict having
> routing expertise too.
>
> In LeSS, we also emphasize that feature teams / developers are customer
> problem solvers instead of efficient solution implementers. Adaptive
> expertise is a requirement for being customer problem solver.
>
> There is much more about this on the wikipedia page:
> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_expertise
>
> I’m quite surprised I haven’t heard about this before.
