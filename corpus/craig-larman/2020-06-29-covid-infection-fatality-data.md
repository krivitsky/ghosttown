to assess risk data is useful... covid infection and fatality data starting
to clarify

the largest and latest meta-analysis estimates a global average infection
fatality rate (IFR) of 0.25%. but noteworthy is the strong fatality skewing
towards people 70+ ... if <70, the global IFR is much lower at 0.04%

the latest cdc data (large usa data set) is similar but suggests an even
"better" IFR for younger people. national average IFR of 0.26. again
fatality skews mostly 70+... the *case* fatality rate (CFR) for ages 0-49
is 0.05 which implies the *infection* fatality rate (IFR) is much lower in
that age group, since it is estimated that only a tiny percentage of people
with infection go on to be recorded as "cases."

italy has (sadly) lots of data and have published a useful risk ratio table
based on N=70K people. how to read it? the group assigned age 60-69 a risk
ratio of 1 (for fatality). then for example if a person aged 40-49 their
relative risk is 0.14 (almost 7 times lower than a 60-69 yr old)

Age 50-59 0.31
