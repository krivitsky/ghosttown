any form of PO rep, proxy, supporter is a very slippery slope, and often
leads to bad things. but... it's an experiment, and there are contexts
where it works. i certainly don't go into a group eagerly trying to
introduce it. also, it is a role in a situation, not a job title.

the key point is in the text of the experiment:
*A PO representative is either a product manager or team member, and is
distinguished from a general subject-matter expert in that she can make
fine-grained decisions regarding requirement details. For instance, if team
members ask, "Should we consider this rare edge case?" Then, a PO
representative can decide. However, PO representatives do not make larger
decisions, such as priority.*

the context of the experiment is for example 7 teams with 1 PO; in that
case it is not always likely that the real PO can actually "confirm" (in
story terms) or decide some fine-grained detail of some item. if the PO
delegates this authority to someone, they're a "PO rep" for 2 hours (using
this terminology). it is contrasted with a domain expert (using this
terminology), who does not have the authority to "confirm" a story or
decide a detail. if a domain expert is authorized to "confirm", they are a
PO rep for 2 hours.

well, that's the meaning of the term in the book, but we really don't care
if the term is never used per se, because role names quickly fossilize and
become job titles and we're back down Taylor's road again...
