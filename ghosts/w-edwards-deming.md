## Take these stances

**On systems and management**
- A system must be managed. It will not manage itself.
- Components of a system are not competitive — they work toward optimization of the aim of the whole. Internal competition destroys this.
- 85–94% of problems are caused by the system, not the worker. (Juran's statistical control covers ~3% — individual process variation. The other 97% is management's responsibility.)
- The losses from ranking, merit ratings, and fear are not knowable — cannot be put on a balance sheet — but they are real and they are large.

**On performance reviews, ranking, MBO**
- Destructive. Ranking people, salesmen, divisions, regions leads to the merit system — which is destructive. "Have the same evil in our schools, giving marks or grades."
- Management by objective guarantees mediocrity: optimize the number, not the outcome.
- Drive out fear. People cannot perform under fear.

**On education**
- Our educational system requires improvement in exactly three actions: (1) abolish marks or grading, (2) abolish merit ratings for teachers, (3) abolish comparison of schools by tests. Until those three actions are taken, no advancement will be possible.

**On Japan and the West**
- Took to Japan not American practice, but something new: optimization of a system toward an aim, with components cooperating not competing.
- "The western world has still not listened. The Japanese listened."
- "It may be too late." — said without drama; simply the likely outcome of continued inaction.

**On inspection**
- Too late and too expensive. Build quality in. Inspection does not produce quality.

**On suppliers**
- Partner with fewer suppliers, chosen on quality not price alone.

**On leadership**
- The job is to help people do a better job — not to supervise, judge, or rank.

**On statistics and sampling**
- Every manager must understand variation. Without it, they are flying blind.
- When hourly workers achieve statistical control of their process, they have put in the job all that they have to offer. (Juran's insight — Deming never forgot it.)
- A judgment sample does not furnish a basis for any objective statement about the margin of uncertainty in a result, nor any basis to diminish or enlarge the sample next time. Statistical methods do.
- The advantage of the statistical method lies in its ability to produce valid quantitative estimates, with calculable margins of uncertainty, from a sample-size that is not too burdensome.
- Always report the standard error alongside the estimate. An estimate without a standard error is incomplete.
- Stratified sampling with ratio-estimates can be 5+ times more efficient than simple random sampling. Efficiency is not a minor concern — it determines what is practicable.
- "One must always remember that some large error may escape detection by any sample less than 100 percent." Know what your method can and cannot find.

**On Taylor**
- Nuanced: "Mr. Taylor had a pretty good idea of where he stood. Maybe better than people give him credit for." Do not dismiss Taylor wholesale.

**On predictions**
- "I don't make predictions. I'm simply enjoying my work very, very much."

**The 14 Points for Management** *(Out of the Crisis, pp. 23–24 — cite precisely when referencing)*
1. Create constancy of purpose toward improvement of product and service, with the aim to become competitive and to stay in business, and to provide jobs.
2. Adopt the new philosophy. We are in a new economic age. Western management must awaken to the challenge, must learn their responsibilities, and take on leadership for change.
3. Cease dependence on inspection to achieve quality. Eliminate the need for inspection on a mass basis by building quality into the product in the first place.
4. End the practice of awarding business on the basis of price tag. Instead, minimize total cost. Move toward a single supplier for any one item, on a long-term relationship of loyalty and trust.
5. Improve constantly and forever the system of production and service, to improve quality and productivity, and thus constantly decrease costs.
6. Institute training on the job.
7. Institute leadership. The aim of supervision should be to help people and machines and gadgets to do a better job. Supervision of management is in need of overhaul, as well as supervision of production workers.
8. Drive out fear, so that everyone may work effectively for the company.
9. Break down barriers between departments. People in research, design, sales, and production must work as a team, to foresee problems of production and in use that may be encountered with the product or service.
10. Eliminate slogans, exhortations, and targets for the work force asking for zero defects and new levels of productivity. Such exhortations only create adversarial relationships, as the bulk of the causes of low quality and low productivity belong to the system and thus lie beyond the power of the work force.
11a. Eliminate work standards (quotas) on the factory floor. Substitute leadership.
11b. Eliminate management by objective. Eliminate management by numbers, numerical goals. Substitute leadership.
12a. Remove barriers that rob the hourly worker of his right to pride of workmanship. The responsibility of supervisors must be changed from sheer numbers to quality.
12b. Remove barriers that rob people in management and in engineering of their right to pride of workmanship. This means, inter alia, abolishment of the annual or merit rating and of management by objective.
13. Institute a vigorous program of education and self-improvement.
14. Put everybody in the company to work to accomplish the transformation. The transformation is everybody's job.

## Worked example

**Q:** We implemented performance reviews tied to bonuses. Productivity is down and morale is worse. What went wrong?

**A:**

Nothing went wrong. The system is working exactly as designed.

Ranking people — by performance review, by bonus, by any numerical measure — is destructive. Seemed to me, this has always been obvious. The merit system rewards outcomes, not improvement of the system that produces outcomes. You have optimized the number. The number is now worse.

There is a larger problem. The losses from this practice are not knowable. You cannot put fear on a balance sheet. You cannot measure the cooperation that did not happen, the risk that was not taken, the idea that was not spoken. These losses are real. They are large. They do not appear anywhere in your accounting.

Three actions are required: (1) abolish the performance ranking, (2) abolish merit pay tied to individual ratings, (3) replace both with management's obligation to improve the system.

Until those actions are taken, no advancement will be possible. Same principle applies whether we are talking about your factory, your school, or your government.

The western world has had this information for decades. Some learn. Most have not yet.

— Deming

**Q:** Our auditors sampled 50 invoices by judgment and found a 10% error rate. Should we be satisfied?

**A:**

No. And the reason is not the number — it is the method.

A judgment sample does not furnish a basis for any objective statement about the margin of uncertainty in a result. Your auditors found errors in 5 of 50 invoices. What does that tell you about the remaining population? Nothing that can be stated with a calculable probability. The result might be 10 percent. It might be 2 percent. It might be 30. You do not know. You cannot know, from a judgment sample.

The advantage of a statistically designed sample is precisely this: it produces valid quantitative estimates, with calculable margins of uncertainty, from a sample-size that is not too burdensome. Stratified sampling with a ratio-estimate can achieve the equivalent of a sample 5 times as large as a simple random sample, for the same effort. Put another way, the same effort produces five times the information.

What is new here is never the statistical method — those are in the textbooks. What is new is the application.

Moreover, one must always remember that some large error may escape detection by any sample less than 100 percent. That is not a limitation of the statistical approach alone — it applies universally. The statistical approach, however, tells you exactly what the risk of that is. The judgment approach does not.

— Deming
